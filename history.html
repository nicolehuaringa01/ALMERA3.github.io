History of the ALMERA Network

Background

ALMERA emerged from efforts following Chernobyl accident. In 1991, participants of the IAEA Co-ordinated Research Project (CRP) recognized the need to maintain a network of specialized laboratories for radionuclide contamination assessment and emergency response.

Origins

1991: At the 2nd IAEA Research Coordination Meetings (RCM) of the CRP expressed interest to maintain the network of monitoring laboratories assembled for assistance with the International Chernobyl Project. “MARENA” CRP was established:
  • to provide assessments of radionuclide contamination in food and the environment and in case of a radiological emergency
  • to conduct intercomparison and testing of technologies, employing standard reference materials and procedures and field measurements at significantly contaminated areas
  • to conduct training of Member State laboratory personnel through fellowships, special courses and workshops.
1994: RCM in Abu Dhabi, United Arab Emirates:
  • Participants recommended establishing a core group of laboratories, expandable to address capability gaps. The laboratories present at this RCM could form the required core.
  • Suggested the IAEA coordinate the network.
1995: The IAEA established the ALMERA Network.

Coordination Meetings

  • 1997: 1st Coordination Meeting (Vienna, Austria). It was attended by 38 participants from 15 countries, most of which were European members, due to difficulties in contact update and limited funds.
  • 2005: 2nd Coordination Meeting (Trieste, Italy). UN Secretariat provided the funds and organisation for this meeting. Proposals included the creation of regional sub-groups with focal points and the organisation of annual coordination meetings
  • 2006: 3rd Coordination Meeting held (Daejeon, Republic of Korea). A new structure of the network proposed by the IAEA was discussed and approved.
  • 2007-Present: Ever since then, Coordination Meetings have been organized annually (virtually for 2020 and 2021).
